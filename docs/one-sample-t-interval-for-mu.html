14 One Sample t-Interval for \(\mu\)

14.1 The t-distribution

Proportions= Normal Distribution (z-distribution)

Confidence Intervals for a population proportion \(p\) are based on z-values from the standard Normal distribution.

Proportions are really simple to work with, because they‚Äôre always standardized‚Äîthe largest decimal is 1, and the smallest is 0. This means that we don‚Äôt have to worry about the spread of the population as much.

Means= \(t\)-distribution

Means can take any value‚Äîthey could be negative, they could be 78, they could be 10,000. As a result, the sampling distribution for means naturally has a larger spread‚Äî this means that the sampling distribution is no longer normal.

When we only know the sample‚Äôs standard deviation \(s_x\)‚Äîso not the population standard deviation \(\sigma\)‚Äîthe distribution takes on a new shape called the t-distribution. You can see a comparison above.

The t-distribution has a much larger spread than the normal distribution does‚Äîthis is to account for the naturally larger spread that sample means would have.

The \(t\)-distributions are a family of symmetric, bell-shaped distributions that are centered at 0, but whose spread is determined by its degrees of freedom. The area of those tails depends on the degrees of freedom the sampling distribution has. The larger the sample, the closer the t-distribution will get to the Normal distribution. The smaller the sample, the greater its varability, and so the t-distribution compenstates by adding more area in its tails.

When we talk about z-scores within the t-distribution (e.g.¬†how many standard deviations a data point is away from the mean), we still use the same formula, but we now call them t-values, to differentiate them from the Normal distribution.

14.2 Finding areas under the \(t\)-distribution

Just like how in 11.4.2 we used pnorm() to find areas given a \(z\)-score, and qnorm() to find \(z\) given a area, we can use pt() and qt() to find the t-distribution.

Find Area,
given \(z\) or \(t\) 		Find \(z\) or \(t\),
given Area
Normal (\(z\)) pnorm() qnorm()
\(t\) pt() qt()

In both cases, R will always calculate the cumulative area starting from the left of the distribution.

To find any area under the \(t\)-distribution, you need the \(t\)-value and the degrees of freedom.

pt(0, df=14)
## [1] 0.5
pt(1.25, df =3)
## [1] 0.85

Similarly, to find a corresponding t-value given an area, use qt().

qt(0.5, df=14)
## [1] 0
qt(0.85, df=3)
## [1] 1.25
